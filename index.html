🧠 Executive Function Demands Are Hidden Curriculum

In My Own Words
Students are not just learning content — they are also learning how to manage time, materials, and attention. These demands are almost never explicitly taught, yet they determine academic success as much as content knowledge does.

Every assignment assumes a level of executive function skill that many students simply don't have yet. Asking a student to "manage their time" on a long-term project assumes they already have the neural infrastructure to plan, monitor progress, and adjust deadlines.

🔥 Why This Matters

If we design assessments that reward existing executive function skills rather than teaching them, we're measuring privilege and development, not learning.

⚡ The Tension

Direct instruction in executive function skills takes classroom time away from content coverage. The solution isn't more minutes; it's integration.

"Executive functions are not a curriculum to add — they are a lens through which to see your existing curriculum."

🔗 Connections

[[Universal Design for Learning]] — UDL's multiple means of engagement directly addresses this gap
[[Assignment Scaffolding]] — breaking long-term projects into graded checkpoints externalizes executive function
